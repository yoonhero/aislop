Definitions 3
  • An ordered ring has a total order compatible with addition and multiplication by positive elements.
  • A positive cone P satisfies closure under +, closure under multiplication, and exactly one of a=0, a in P, -a in P.

Theorems 5

Positive cone equivalence

Giving an order compatible with ring operations is equivalent to giving a positive cone P with the three cone properties.

Proof spine. From an order take P={a:a>0}. From P define \(a

Squares are nonnegative

In an ordered ring, a^2>=0 for every a, and if a != 0 then a^2>0.

Proof spine. If a>0, closure gives a^2>0. If a<0, then -a>0 and a^2=(-a)^2>0.

Ordered domains have characteristic 0

An ordered ring with unity and no collapse of order has characteristic 0.

Proof spine. 1>0, so n*1 is positive for every n>0 and cannot be 0.

Order transport by isomorphism

A ring isomorphism from an ordered ring transports the positive cone and hence the order to the target ring.

Proof spine. Declare y positive when y=phi(x) for some positive x; cone axioms follow from the homomorphism laws.

Ordering the quotient field

The field of quotients of an ordered integral domain has a natural compatible ordering extending the original one.

Proof spine. Define \(a/b\) positive when ab is positive after choosing b with sign accounted for; check this is independent of representatives.

Propositions 2

No finite ordered fields

A finite field cannot be ordered.

Proof spine. Finite fields have positive prime characteristic, but ordered fields have characteristic 0.

-1 is not a square

In an ordered field, -1 cannot be a square.

Proof spine. Squares are nonnegative while -1<0.

Examples 4

Q and R

The usual orders on Q and R make them ordered fields.

Use. standard model.

C is not ordered

C cannot be ordered as a field because i^2=-1.

Use. one-line obstruction.

Polynomial rings

\(R[x]\) can be ordered by declaring the leading coefficient of a polynomial positive.

Use. order need not come from evaluating at a real number.

Ordering Q from Z

The usual order on Z extends to the usual order on Q by the quotient-field ordering theorem.

Use. This is the prototype for ordered domains.

Exercises 6

Z_p obstruction

Show \(\mathbb Z_p\) cannot be an ordered field.

Proof spine. Use characteristic p.

Square argument

Prove that if F is ordered then \(x^2+1\) has no root in F.

Proof spine. x^2>=0.

Cone check

Given P in Q as positive rationals, verify the cone axioms.

Proof spine. Closure and trichotomy.

Order transport

If F is ordered and K is isomorphic to F, define an order on K.

Proof spine. Pull back the positive cone through the isomorphism.

Transport order

If phi:R->S is a ring isomorphism and R is ordered, write the positive cone of S.

Proof spine. \(P_S=\varphi(P_R)\).

Quotient field sign

In an ordered domain, explain why \(a/b\) and (-a)/(-b) get the same sign.

Proof spine. Their cross-products represent the same fraction and the sign rule is representative-independent.

Proof Patterns 2
  • Ordered-field impossibility usually follows from characteristic or -1 square obstruction.
  • Use P rather than informal sign language when proving abstract order facts.
