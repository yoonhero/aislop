Definitions 6
  • A monomial order is a total order on power products compatible with multiplication.
  • The leading term \(\operatorname{LT}(f)\) is the largest term of f under the chosen order.
  • A Groebner basis G for I is a generating set whose leading terms generate all leading terms of elements of I.
  • The \(S\text{-polynomial}\) of f and g cancels their leading terms using the least common multiple of leading monomials.
  • The algebraic variety \(V(S)\) is the set of common zeros of a finite set S of polynomials.
  • A basis of an ideal I is a finite set of polynomials that generates I as an ideal.
Theorems 5

Division by a list

Given an ordered list of polynomials, a polynomial f can be reduced to a remainder not divisible by any leading term in the list.

Proof spine. Repeatedly cancel the current leading term when possible. Term order prevents infinite descent.

Groebner membership test

If G is a Groebner basis for I, then f lies in I iff the remainder of f on division by G is 0.

Proof spine. If remainder r is nonzero and f in I, then LT(r) should be divisible by some LT(g), contradicting reducedness.

Buchberger criterion

A generating set G is a Groebner basis iff every \(S\text{-polynomial}\) S(g_i,g_j) reduces to 0 modulo G.

Proof spine. The criterion detects whether all leading-term conflicts have been resolved.

Hilbert basis theorem

Every ideal in \(F[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\) has a finite basis.

Proof spine. The proof is omitted here; its role is to guarantee finite data behind polynomial ideals.

Common zeros depend on ideal

A set of polynomials and the ideal it generates have the same common zero set.

Proof spine. If every generator vanishes at a point, every polynomial combination of them also vanishes there.

Propositions 2

Lex elimination signal

With lex order, Groebner bases often contain polynomials involving fewer variables, enabling elimination.

Proof spine. Leading terms prioritize variables, forcing reductions to remove the larger variables first.

One-variable case

In \(F[x]\), a single generator of an ideal is already a Groebner basis.

Proof spine. The division algorithm gives the membership test.

Examples 5

Membership

To test f in (g1,g2), reduce f by a Groebner basis for the ideal. Remainder 0 means yes; nonzero means no.

Use. ordinary generators may not be enough unless they are Groebner.

Simple basis

In \(F[x,y]\) with lex order x>y, {x-y, y^2-1} lets you eliminate x and solve y^2=1 first.

Use. system solving becomes triangular.

Why S-polynomials appear

If \(\operatorname{LT}(f)\) and LT(g) overlap, their leading terms can cancel in a combination. \(S\text{-polynomial}\) tests whether that cancellation creates a new leading term.

Use. Buchberger is conflict resolution.

Variety of a line

For S={2x+y-2} in R[x,y], \(V(S)\) is the line \(2x+y=2\).

Use. A variety is the geometric shadow of equations.

Ideal basis versus Groebner basis

A generating set can be a basis of an ideal without being a Groebner basis.

Use. Groebner basis is a stronger computational basis.

Exercises 6

Reduce

Reduce f=x^2y+xy by G={x-y} under lex x>y.

Proof spine. Replace x by y step by step; final expression is in y.

Membership idea

Explain why a nonzero remainder proves f notin I when G is Groebner.

Proof spine. Use the leading-term contradiction.

S-polynomial

Write the \(S\text{-polynomial}\) formula for f and g using lcm of leading monomials.

Proof spine. S = l/\(\operatorname{LT}(f)\) * f - l/LT(g) * g, up to leading coefficients.

One-variable

Why is every ideal in \(F[x]\) computationally easy compared with \(F[x,y]\)?

Proof spine. Division by the gcd/single generator controls membership.

Same variety

Show V(f,g)=V((f,g)) for two polynomials f,g.

Proof spine. Use closure under polynomial combinations.

Groebner need

Explain why Hilbert basis theorem alone does not give an algorithm for ideal membership.

Proof spine. It gives finite generation, not a reduction procedure.

Proof Patterns 2
  • Term order arguments end because leading monomials strictly decrease.
  • Membership proof is always: nonzero reduced remainder would have an impossible leading term.
