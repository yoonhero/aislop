Definitions 4
  • The degree \([E:F]\) of a field extension is dim_F E.
  • A finite extension has finite degree.
  • An algebraic extension is one in which every element of E is algebraic over F.
  • A field is algebraically closed if every nonconstant polynomial over it has a root in it.
Theorems 7

Tower law

If \(F\le K\le E\) and the relevant degrees are finite, then \([E:F]=[E:K][K:F]\).

Proof spine. Multiply a basis of K over F with a basis of E over K; products form a basis of E over F.

Finite extensions are algebraic

If \([E:F]\) is finite, then every element of E is algebraic over F.

Proof spine. For alpha in E, the list 1,alpha,...,alpha^n is linearly dependent once n=\([E:F]\), producing a polynomial relation.

Algebraic elements form a field

Inside an extension E of F, the set of all elements algebraic over F is a subfield of E.

Proof spine. For alpha,beta algebraic, \(F(\alpha,\beta)\) is finite over F; sums, products, negatives, inverses lie in that finite extension and are algebraic.

Algebraically closed criterion

A field F is algebraically closed iff every nonconstant polynomial in \(F[x]\) splits into linear factors over F.

Proof spine. A root gives a linear factor; repeat by induction on degree.

Algebraic closure exists

Every field has an algebraic closure: an algebraic extension that is algebraically closed.

Proof spine. The proof uses a maximality argument; keep the statement as a structural endpoint.

Fundamental theorem of algebra

The field C of complex numbers is algebraically closed.

Proof spine. The usual proof is analytic; algebraically, the key consequence is that every complex polynomial splits into linear factors.

No proper algebraic extension of algebraically closed field

If F is algebraically closed, then it has no proper algebraic extension.

Proof spine. An algebraic element over F has a minimal polynomial, which must be linear over an algebraically closed field.

Propositions 2

Divisibility of degrees

If beta lies in \(F(\alpha)\), then [F(beta):F] divides [\(F(\alpha)\):F].

Proof spine. Use F <= F(beta) <= \(F(\alpha)\) and tower law.

Algebraic over algebraic

If E is algebraic over K and K is algebraic over F, then E is algebraic over F.

Proof spine. Each element is contained in a finite extension generated by finitely many algebraic coefficients.

Examples 4

Q(sqrt2,sqrt3)

The degree is 4: \(\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)\) has basis {1,\(\sqrt2\),\(\sqrt3\),sqrt6} \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\).

Use. tower law plus noncontainment.

Nested radicals

Q(\(\sqrt2\), \(\sqrt[3]{2}\)) has degree 6 if \(x^3-2\) remains irreducible over \(\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)\).

Use. multiply degrees when the second polynomial remains irreducible.

Algebraic numbers

All complex numbers algebraic \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\) form a field.

Use. closure under field operations is nontrivial and useful.

C versus R

C is algebraically closed, while R is not because \(x^2+1\) has no root in R.

Use. This is the shortest contrast between algebraically closed and not.

Exercises 5

Tower law use

If \([E:K]\)=3 and \([K:F]\)=4, find \([E:F]\).

Proof spine. 12.

Finite implies algebraic

Produce the polynomial relation for alpha in a 3-dimensional extension.

Proof spine. The four vectors 1,alpha,alpha^2,alpha^3 are dependent.

Degree divisibility

Can \(\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)\) contain \(\sqrt[3]{2}\)?

Proof spine. No, degree 3 would divide degree 2 if it did.

Algebraically closed

Why is R not algebraically closed?

Proof spine. \(x^2+1\) has no real root.

Algebraically closed consequence

If F is algebraically closed and alpha is algebraic over F, prove alpha in F.

Proof spine. The minimal polynomial of alpha over F is linear.

Proof Patterns 2
  • When stuck on algebraicity of a combination, put all ingredients in one finite extension.
  • Degree impossibility is often divisibility via tower law.
