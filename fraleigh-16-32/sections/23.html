Definitions 3
  • A nonconstant polynomial p in \(F[x]\) is irreducible if p=ab implies a or b is constant.
  • Two factorizations are the same up to order and nonzero scalar factors.
  • A prime polynomial p divides ab only when p divides a or p divides b.
Theorems 6

Euclidean gcd theorem

In \(F[x]\), gcds exist and can be expressed as sf+tg for polynomials s,t.

Proof spine. Use the division algorithm exactly as in the Euclidean algorithm for integers.

Irreducible implies prime in F[x]

If p is irreducible in \(F[x]\) and p divides ab, then p divides a or p divides b.

Proof spine. If p does not divide a, gcd(p,a)=1; Bezout gives sp+ta=1. Multiply by b.

Unique factorization in F[x]

Every nonconstant polynomial in \(F[x]\) factors into irreducibles, and the factorization is unique up to order and units.

Proof spine. Existence by induction on degree. Uniqueness by irreducible implies prime.

Gauss-type reducibility over Q

A polynomial in \(\mathbb Z[x]\) that factors \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\) into lower-degree polynomials also factors over Z after clearing unit factors appropriately.

Proof spine. Clear denominators and use primitive parts; this moves rational factorization back to integer coefficients.

Rational zero theorem

If a monic or integer polynomial has a rational zero m/n in lowest terms, then numerator and denominator divide the appropriate constant and leading coefficients.

Proof spine. Substitute m/n and compare divisibility after multiplying by n^d.

Eisenstein criterion

If a prime p divides every coefficient except the leading coefficient, p does not divide the leading coefficient, and p^2 does not divide the constant term, then the polynomial is irreducible \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\).

Proof spine. Assume a factorization in \(\mathbb Z[x]\); reduction of coefficients forces both constant terms to be divisible by p, contradicting the p^2 condition.

Propositions 2

Degree 2 or 3 criterion

A polynomial over F of degree 2 or 3 is reducible iff it has a root in F.

Proof spine. A proper factorization must include a linear factor.

Root bound

A nonzero polynomial of degree n over a field has at most n roots.

Proof spine. Each root contributes a factor \(x-a\); degree limits how many can occur.

Examples 5

x^2+x+1 over Z_2

Evaluate at 0 and 1. Both are nonzero, so the quadratic is irreducible over \(\mathbb Z_2\).

Use. This polynomial constructs the field with four elements later.

x^4-1 over Q

\(x^4-1\)=(x-1)(x+1)(\(x^2+1\)), and \(x^2+1\) is irreducible \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\).

Use. factorization depends on the base field.

Finite subgroup of a field

A finite subgroup of F* is cyclic.

Use. This result explains multiplicative groups of finite fields later.

Eisenstein example

\(x^5+6x+3\) is irreducible \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\) by Eisenstein with p=3.

Use. A fast irreducibility certificate.

Rational zero example

For \(x^3-2\), the rational zero theorem leaves possible rational roots ±1, ±2; none work, so the cubic is irreducible \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\).

Use. Degree 3 plus no rational root gives irreducible.

Exercises 6

Irreducible cubic

Determine whether x^3+x+1 is irreducible over \(\mathbb Z_2\).

Proof spine. Check roots 0 and 1.

Root bound proof

Prove a degree n polynomial has at most n roots.

Proof spine. Induct using the factor theorem.

Factor over fields

Factor \(x^2-2\) \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\) and over R.

Proof spine. Irreducible \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\), linear factors over R.

GCD

Find gcd(x^2-1, \(x^2-2x+1\)) in \(\mathbb Q[x]\).

Proof spine. Common factor is x-1.

Use Eisenstein

Prove \(x^4+10x^2+5\) is irreducible \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\).

Proof spine. Use p=5.

Rational root list

List all possible rational roots of \(2x^3-3x+6\).

Proof spine. Numerators divide 6 and denominators divide 2.

Proof Patterns 2
  • Irreducibility is always relative to the field.
  • For uniqueness proofs, use Bezout to turn irreducible into prime.
