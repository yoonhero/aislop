Definitions 3
  • A real number is constructible if it appears as a coordinate of a point constructible from 0 and 1 by straightedge and compass.
  • Constructible numbers form a subfield of R.
  • A quadratic extension is obtained by adjoining a square root of an element already present.
Theorems 5

Field operations are constructible

If \(\alpha\text{ and }\beta\) are constructible, then \(\alpha+\beta\), \(\alpha-\beta\), \(\alpha\beta\), and \(\alpha/\beta\) for \(\beta\ne0\) are constructible.

Proof spine. Classical line constructions implement arithmetic on a fixed coordinate line.

Square roots are constructible

If \(\alpha>0\) is constructible, then \(\sqrt\alpha\) is constructible.

Proof spine. Use similar triangles or circle construction to realize geometric mean.

Constructible degree obstruction

If \(\alpha\text{ is constructible}\) and algebraic \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\), then \([\mathbb Q(\alpha):\mathbb Q]\) is a power of 2 divisor; more precisely \(\alpha\text{ lies}\) in a tower of quadratic extensions \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\).

Proof spine. Every new intersection point solves at most a quadratic equation over the previous coordinate field.

Constructible iff quadratic tower

A real algebraic number is constructible only if it lies in a field obtained from Q by finitely many quadratic extensions; conversely elements built by such field operations and square roots are constructible.

Proof spine. Line and circle intersections solve equations of degree at most two over the previous coordinate field.

Regular polygon criterion

A regular n-gon is constructible exactly when \(\cos(2\pi/n)\) is constructible; the full classification leads to Fermat-prime conditions.

Proof spine. Constructing the central angle and constructing the polygon are equivalent tasks.

Propositions 2

Line-circle algebra

Coordinates of intersections of constructible lines and circles are obtained by solving linear or quadratic equations over the previous field.

Proof spine. Line equations are linear; circle equations are quadratic.

Non-power-of-two obstruction

If the minimal polynomial degree of alpha \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\) is not a power of 2, then alpha is not constructible.

Proof spine. The degree would need to divide a power of 2 by tower law.

Examples 4

Doubling the cube

\(\sqrt[3]{2}\) has minimal polynomial \(x^3-2\) \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\), degree 3, so it is not constructible.

Use. degree 3 is not compatible with quadratic towers.

Angle trisection

Trisecting a general 60-degree angle would construct \(\cos20^\circ\) degrees, which satisfies a cubic obstruction.

Use. not every angle can be trisected.

Squaring the circle

A square with area pi would construct sqrt(pi), which would make pi algebraic/constructible; pi is transcendental, so impossible.

Use. this uses a deeper fact about pi.

Regular polygons

Regular 3-, 4-, 5-, 6-, 8-, 10-, 12-, 15-, 16-, 17-, 20-, and 30-gons fit the constructible pattern discussed in the exercises.

Use. Polygon questions are constructibility questions for angles.

Exercises 6

Constructible closure

Show constructible numbers are closed under subtraction.

Proof spine. Use field operation theorem or reflect lengths on the line.

Degree test

Can a root of an irreducible quintic \(\text{over }\mathbb Q\) be constructible?

Proof spine. Not if its degree is 5.

sqrt tower

Explain why \(\sqrt{1+\sqrt2}\) is constructible.

Proof spine. Start with \(\sqrt2\), then add 1, then take another square root.

Cube duplication

Formalize the obstruction for constructing a cube with twice the volume.

Proof spine. The side length would be \(\sqrt[3]{2}\).

Regular 9-gon

Use the cubic obstruction behind Theorem 32.11 to show the regular 9-gon is not constructible.

Proof spine. Relate it to trisecting a 120-degree angle or to the degree of the relevant cosine.

Regular 10-gon

Show a regular 10-gon is constructible.

Proof spine. Use that a regular 5-gon is constructible and bisect the central angle.

Proof Patterns 2
  • Translate geometry into coordinates, then coordinates into field extensions.
  • Impossibility proofs need algebraic degree evidence; pictures alone do not prove impossibility.
