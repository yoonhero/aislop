Definitions 4
  • A ring homomorphism preserves addition and multiplication.
  • \(\ker\varphi=\{r\in R\mid\varphi(r)=0\}\).
  • An ideal N of R is an additive subgroup such that rN and Nr lie in N for all r in R.
  • The factor ring \(R/N\) consists of additive cosets r+N with induced addition and multiplication.
Theorems 4

Kernel ideal theorem

The kernel of a ring homomorphism phi:\(R\to R'\) is an ideal of R.

Proof spine. Additive subgroup follows from group homomorphism. If a in \(\ker\varphi\), then phi(ra)=phi(r)phi(a)=0 and similarly phi(ar)=0.

Factor ring construction

If N is an ideal of R, then \((a+N)(b+N)=ab+N\) is well-defined and makes \(R/N\) a ring.

Proof spine. Changing representatives adds terms in N: an, mb, nm. Ideal absorption keeps them in N.

Fundamental homomorphism theorem

For a ring homomorphism phi:\(R\to R'\), \(R/\ker\varphi\) is isomorphic to the image phi[R].

Proof spine. Map r+\(\ker\varphi\) to phi(r). The kernel condition is exactly the well-definedness condition.

Coset multiplication criterion

For a subring H of R, multiplication on additive cosets by \((a+H)(b+H)=ab+H\) is well-defined exactly when H absorbs multiplication as an ideal.

Proof spine. Representative changes introduce ah, hb, and h1h2 terms; these must always lie in H.

Propositions 2

Natural projection

The map \(\pi:R\to R/N\), \(\pi(r)=r+N\), is a surjective ring homomorphism with kernel N.

Proof spine. Directly check operations and pi(r)=N iff r in N.

Ideal test in commutative rings

In a commutative ring, an additive subgroup N is an ideal iff r n in N for all r in R and n in N.

Proof spine. Left and right absorption coincide.

Examples 4

Z/nZ

The map Z->\(\mathbb Z_n\) has kernel \(n\mathbb Z\), so \(\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z\) ≅ \(\mathbb Z_n\).

Use. the prototype quotient ring.

Evaluation quotient

For phi_a:\(F[x]\)->F, phi_a(f)=f(a), the kernel is (\(x-a\)), so \(F[x]/(x-a)\)≅F.

Use. root/factor theorem as homomorphism theorem.

Function vanishing ideal

Continuous or arbitrary functions vanishing at a point form an ideal in a function ring.

Use. geometric intuition: quotient remembers the value at that point.

Factor ring Z/nZ

Since \(n\mathbb Z\) is an ideal of Z, the additive cosets form the ring \(\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z\).

Use. This connects factor rings back to modular arithmetic.

Exercises 5

Kernel

Find the kernel of phi:\(\mathbb Z[x]\)->Z, phi(f)=f(0).

Proof spine. Polynomials with constant term 0, i.e. (x).

Well-defined

Show multiplication in \(R/N\) is well-defined when N is an ideal.

Proof spine. Expand (a+n)(b+m)-ab.

Not ideal

Give a subring of a ring that is not an ideal.

Proof spine. Z as scalar matrices? Easier: diagonal matrices inside \(M_2(F)\) are not ideal.

Image quotient

Compute \(\mathbb R[x]/(x^2+1)\) over R as a familiar field.

Proof spine. It is isomorphic to C.

Ideal necessity

Find where the proof of quotient multiplication fails if H is merely a subring.

Proof spine. The cross terms ah and hb need not lie in H.

Proof Patterns 2
  • For quotient multiplication, always compare two representatives and show the difference is in N.
  • For homomorphism theorem, define the map on cosets and prove well-definedness first.
